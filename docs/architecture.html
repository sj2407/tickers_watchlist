A watchlist tool: the data and the thinking

01

Overview

The tool tracks a watchlist and gives one recommendation per name: add, hold, trim, or exit. Fixed rules compute that recommendation from market and fundamental data; a model writes the reasoning and makes the final exit call. The path from data to a recommendation runs in four stages.

1 · Raw inputs
  • Prices, highs, lows, volume
  • Revenue, EPS, gross margin
  • News and earnings dates
  • Analyst buy / hold / sell counts
▸
2 · Metrics
  • RSI, MACD, trend, moving averages
  • Returns and relative strength
  • Revenue and EPS growth, margins
  • Support, resistance, volume
▸
3 · Signals
  • Uptrend / downtrend
  • Overbought / extended
  • Revenue weakening
  • Leading or lagging the market
▸
4 · Recommendation
  • Watch, add, hold, or trim
  • Trim can become exit
  • The reasons behind the recommendation

Only the last stage uses the model, and only to write the read and to judge an exit. Everything before it is computed from data, the same way every time.

03

Metrics

From the raw inputs the pipeline computes a fixed set of metrics, in six groups.

Trend & momentum

  • RSI (14) compares the average size of up moves to down moves over the last 14 trading days on a scale from 0 to 100, so it captures whether a stock has run too hot after a rally (above 70, overbought) or been sold off too hard (below 30, oversold).
  • MACD tracks the gap between a stock's 12-day and 26-day moving averages against a 9-day average of that gap, so when the lines cross it signals that short-term momentum is turning up or down relative to the recent trend.
  • The 50 / 200-day cross compares the 50-day moving average with the 200-day; the faster line rising above the slower one is a golden cross that marks a shift into a longer-term uptrend, and falling below it is a death cross that marks the opposite.
  • Trend reads where the price sits relative to its own averages: an uptrend means the price is above its 50-day average and the 50-day is above the 200-day, so the medium and long-term directions agree.
  • Distance to the averages measures how far above or below its 20, 50, and 200-day averages the price is trading, which shows whether the stock is stretched far from its recent base or sitting close to it.
  • Distance from the 52-week high is how far below its highest close of the past year the stock is trading, a quick read on whether it is near its peak or well off it.

Price levels & conviction

  • Support and resistance are recent price levels where the stock has repeatedly stopped falling or stopped rising; we read them from local lows and highs on the chart, and they mark the levels a move needs to hold or break.
  • Relative volume divides the day's trading volume by the 20-day average, so a value well above one shows unusually heavy trading and stronger conviction behind the move.
  • ATR%, the average true range as a percent of price, measures how much the stock typically moves in a single day, which puts any given swing in context for that particular name.
  • A breakout flags when the price closes above its highest level of the prior 20 days, and we count it as confirmed only when volume runs at least twice its recent average.

Fundamentals

  • Revenue growth compares the latest quarter's revenue to the same quarter a year earlier, showing whether the business is still expanding its sales and how quickly.
  • EPS growth compares the latest quarter's earnings per share to the same quarter a year earlier, showing whether profit per share is keeping pace with revenue.
  • Gross margin is the share of each sales dollar left after the direct cost of making the product, a basic read on pricing power and efficiency.
  • P/E against its own history compares the stock's current price-to-earnings ratio with its range over recent years, showing whether it is expensive or cheap relative to how the market has usually valued it.

Returns & relative strength

  • Returns over 1, 5, and 20 days are the stock's percentage price changes over the last day, week, and roughly month, showing performance across three time frames.
  • Relative strength subtracts the S&P 500's return from the stock's own return over the same window, so a positive value means it is leading the market and a sustained negative value means it is lagging.

Thesis-break flags

  • Revenue rolling over flags when revenue falls 5 percent or more from the prior quarter, an early sign that growth is stalling rather than simply slowing.
  • Margin compression flags when gross margin falls 2 percentage points or more from the prior quarter, which can point to rising costs or weakening pricing power.
  • Repeated EPS misses flags when the company has come in below estimates in at least two of the last three quarters, a sign of recurring execution problems.

Event & analyst

  • Days to earnings counts the days until the next scheduled earnings report, the single biggest event risk on a stock's calendar.
  • Analyst consensus tallies how many Wall Street analysts rate the stock a buy, a hold, or a sell, a quick gauge of professional sentiment.
04

Reasoning

A recommendation comes from fixed rules over those metrics, plus one judgment the model is allowed to make. The rules are transparent and the model's authority is bounded on purpose.

The rules propose

Fixed, traceable rules read the metrics and output watch, add, hold, or trim. The rules never read position size, and trim is the strongest they go.

The model judges the exit

Only the model can turn a trim into a full exit, weighing the news, the guidance, and how severe and persistent the break is. It writes the read; it never changes a number. This keeps a couple of noisy quarters from mechanically forcing an exit.

The decision tree

The order matters: deterioration is checked before strength, so a name that is both strong and breaking down is never read as a buy.

The five deterioration signals

This is the part the recommendation hinges on. A trim requires at least two of these five, each counted once so correlated readings do not stack. A single signal keeps the name at hold.

1Downtrend
Price is below the 50-day average with the 50-day below the 200-day, or the 50-day has crossed below the 200-day (a death cross).
2Lagging the market
The name's 20-day return minus the S&P 500's 20-day return is negative.
3Revenue weakening
Year-over-year revenue growth has turned negative, or revenue fell 5% or more versus the prior quarter.
4Margin compression
Gross margin fell 2 percentage points or more versus the prior quarter. Ignored for fast growers (revenue up 30%+ year over year) unless the drop is 5 points or more.
5Earnings quality slipping
Year-over-year EPS growth has turned negative, or the company missed estimates in 2 of the last 3 quarters.
Two or more of the five, then trim
The other two gates. To propose add, a name needs an uptrend or a golden cross, positive relative strength, and room: not overbought (RSI below 70), not extended (under 12% above the 20-day average), not within a day of earnings, and no deterioration signal. When a held name is strong but overbought, extended, or reporting within a day, the recommendation stays at hold rather than add, so the tool never chases a stretched price.
05

Safeguards

The point of the design is control over what gets ingested. Several rules decide what is allowed in, and they are enforced in code rather than left to good intentions.

✓No facts from memory
Every number comes from a dataset or a live source. The model is never allowed to supply a figure itself.
✓The read is grounded
The model's written view must be backed by the fetched data and current, cited news or filings, not general knowledge.
✓Freshness is checked
Cached data carries a timestamp. Past its window it is bypassed and refetched, so a stale figure never reaches the board.
✓Every figure is dated
Each number is tagged with the date it is true as of, so nothing reads as fresher than it is.
✓One source of truth for meaning
Every metric maps to a single registry entry with its definition, formula, and source. The glossary is generated from that same registry, and a test fails if the two ever drift.
✓The model cannot touch numbers
It writes prose and may escalate one recommendation to exit. It cannot change a computed value or invent a metric.
✓Reproducible by construction
The same inputs always produce the same metrics, so a run can be re-run and checked. The build is tested first, and the design and code were each reviewed by two independent agents.
