## Critical materials   `theme: rare-earth materials`
**[ACCENT: amber]**

**The field.** Rare earths are a group of 17 metals. Their most important product is the permanent
magnet (a magnet that stays magnetized on its own), specifically the NdFeB magnet
(neodymium-iron-boron, the strongest type made). These magnets are small but essential: they make
the electric motors spin in EVs, drones, wind turbines, robots, and missiles. The strategic problem
is concentration. China refines roughly 90% of high-performance NdFeB magnets and controls about
58% of mined supply, which lets it set prices and use export limits as leverage. Western governments
want a home-grown alternative, which is the entire reason this is an investable theme.

#### MP Materials (MP)
- **Role.** Owns Mountain Pass, the only large rare-earth mine in the United States. It mines and
  refines rare earths and is building factories to turn them into finished magnets, moving up from
  raw material to higher-value product.
- **Revenue source.** Today it mostly sells rare-earth concentrate and refined NdPr oxide
  (neodymium-praseodymium oxide, the processed feedstock that magnets are made from). The bet is
  earning far more per ton by making the finished magnets itself. The driver is magnet demand plus
  the Western push to stop depending on China.
- **Competitive landscape.** China dominates: about 90% of magnet output and 58% of mined supply,
  with that share projected to fall toward 69% by 2030 as Western supply grows. MP's new Fort Worth
  plant is expected to make about 1,000 tons of NdFeB magnets by the end of 2025, which is under 1%
  of China's output. So MP is tiny globally, but it is the leading magnet maker outside China, and
  the US Department of Defense backs it with over 400 million dollars of investment and a guaranteed
  minimum price for its material.
- **Moat.** Strategic and political, not commercial scale. Its advantages are being the only scaled
  US rare-earth mine, integrating forward into magnets, and US government backing including a price
  floor that limits its downside. Against China's scale it has no commercial moat; its value is that
  it exists at all as a Western source.
- **Indicators to watch.** NdPr prices, the magnet-plant production ramp, government and customer
  contracts, and China export-policy moves.
- **Catalysts.** China export restrictions (which raise prices and urgency), magnet-plant
  milestones, and new customers in autos and defense.
- **Worst / Base / Best.** Rare-earth prices stay low because China floods supply, and the magnet
  ramp slips, so cash burns. / The magnet plants ramp with government and customer backing and MP
  becomes the credible Western supplier. / Supply-security urgency plus rising magnet demand give it
  real pricing power as a strategic Western champion.
- **Supply links.** None to your book. Its magnets go to EV, defense, and robotics makers outside
  your holdings. A standalone name.

## Quantum computing   `theme: quantum computing`
**[ACCENT: violet]**

**The field.** A normal computer stores information as bits, each a 0 or a 1. A quantum computer
uses qubits (quantum bits), which can represent 0 and 1 at the same time, so the machine can explore
many possibilities at once. In theory this lets it solve specific problems, such as simulating
molecules for drug discovery or certain optimization problems, that no normal computer could finish
in a reasonable time. It is very early. Today's machines are small and error-prone, and the central
challenge is error correction: bundling many shaky physical qubits into one reliable "logical"
qubit. The field also splits by technology. Superconducting qubits (supercooled circuits, used by
Google, IBM, and Rigetti) are the most mature at scale. Trapped-ion qubits (made from single charged
atoms held in place by lasers, used by IonQ and Quantinuum) are slower but more accurate, which is
measured as fidelity (how error-free each operation is).

#### IonQ (IONQ)
- **Role.** Builds trapped-ion quantum computers and sells access to them over the cloud. It is a
  pure-play bet on quantum reaching usefulness.
- **Revenue source.** Research contracts, government programs, and paid cloud access (through Amazon
  Braket and Microsoft Azure). Revenue was about 130 million dollars in 2025, with guidance of 225
  to 245 million for 2026. It is not profitable; the real payoff, if it comes, is years away.
- **Competitive landscape.** The largest pure-play quantum company by revenue, but it competes with
  far deeper-pocketed giants and specialists. Google is ahead on demonstrating raw quantum advantage,
  IBM is ahead on ecosystem and industrial roadmap, and Quantinuum is ahead on error-corrected
  logical qubits, while IonQ leads on trapped-ion fidelity, with a reported two-qubit fidelity above
  99.99%. It paid about 1.08 billion dollars in 2025 to acquire Oxford Ionics, a rival trapped-ion
  developer, to extend that lead.
- **Moat.** Weak and unproven, which is normal for the field. Its edge is high-fidelity trapped-ion
  technology, its patents, and early commercial access, but no company has a durable moat in a
  technology that has not yet become useful, and it is heavily outspent by Google and IBM. This is a
  technology bet, not a defended business.
- **Indicators to watch.** Hardware milestones (qubit count, fidelity, and progress on
  error-corrected logical qubits), contract wins, and cash burn and share dilution.
- **Catalysts.** A credible step toward useful, error-corrected machines, and large government or
  enterprise deals.
- **Worst / Base / Best.** Milestones slip and quantum stays a science project while dilution
  mounts. / Steady milestone progress and government and enterprise pilots fund the journey. /
  Quantum reaches early commercial usefulness and IonQ's approach leads, so the optionality pays off.
- **Supply links.** None. A standalone name.

## 3D sensing and lidar   `theme: lidar`
**[ACCENT: teal]**

**The field.** Lidar (light detection and ranging) fires rapid laser pulses and times how long they
take to bounce back, building a precise 3D map of the surroundings. It gives machines depth
perception, so they can see shape and distance, not just a flat image. Uses run from robots and
industrial automation to trucks, smart infrastructure, and self-driving cars. The market is growing
fast, roughly 3.3 billion dollars in 2026 heading toward 7.8 billion by 2031, but adoption timelines
are uncertain and it is crowded, especially with Chinese makers competing hard on price.

#### Ouster (OUST)
- **Role.** Makes digital lidar sensors (built around a standard chip rather than many discrete
  parts, which lowers cost and helps them scale) for robotics, industrial automation, trucking, and
  infrastructure, not only cars. It merged with its former rival Velodyne in 2023 to gain scale.
- **Revenue source.** Sells sensors, increasingly bundled with software. Revenue was about 67
  million dollars in 2024, growing roughly 30% in 2025. It is still small and burning cash. The
  driver is automation spreading across industries.
- **Competitive landscape.** Crowded and led by Chinese firms. Hesai holds roughly 33 to 37% of
  automotive lidar and RoboSense about 22%, with Chinese makers together around 60% of automotive
  lidar revenue. Western rivals include Luminar (high-performance automotive) and Innoviz. Ouster
  trails the Chinese leaders in automotive but differentiates by serving many industries beyond cars.
- **Moat.** Weak. Its edge is a chip-based digital-lidar design that can scale in cost and the extra
  scale from the Velodyne merger. But lidar is competitive and commoditizing, switching costs are
  low, and Chinese price pressure is intense, so it has no strong moat.
- **Indicators to watch.** Unit shipments, revenue growth, gross margin (the path to profit), design
  wins in robotics and industrial, and cash burn.
- **Catalysts.** An adoption inflection in robotics and industrial automation, large design wins, and
  reaching profitability.
- **Worst / Base / Best.** Adoption stays slow while price wars and cash burn pressure it. / Robotics
  and industrial demand grow steadily and scale improves its economics. / Lidar becomes standard
  across robotics, autonomy, and infrastructure and Ouster is a scaled leader.
- **Supply links.** None to your book. A standalone name.
